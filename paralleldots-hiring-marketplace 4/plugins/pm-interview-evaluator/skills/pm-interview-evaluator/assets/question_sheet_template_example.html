Resume deep-dive — gaps, inconsistencies & standout claims

Ask these first. Highest-signal block: these questions come straight off his resume, so they're the fastest way to see whether the paper holds up.
Employment gap
1There's a gap between August 2021 and July 2022 on your resume — walk me through that period, month to month.
Strong answerGives a clear, consistent account — upskilling, health, family, a venture that didn't work out, a slow market — and does it calmly, without over-justifying. Treats the gap as a fact to explain rather than a secret to defend, and the dates line up with what's on the page.
Red flagsThe account is vague or shifts as you press on the months. Watch for defensiveness, over-explaining something simple, or a story that doesn't reconcile with the dates listed on the resume.
Progression / possible title inflation
2You went from Business Analyst to Senior Program Manager in about three years. At each step, what specifically changed in what you owned — budget, headcount, decisions you could make alone?
Strong answerPoints to concrete growth at each step — larger or harder accounts, more autonomy, decisions he could make without sign-off, real budget or headcount. He can name what got harder, not just what got retitled.
Red flagsThe title moved but the described work sounds identical to the junior role. He talks only in responsibilities, never in decisions he owned, and can't say what specifically expanded when the title changed.
Standout number — revenue
3Your resume says you "drove 42% revenue growth for the enterprise segment." How was that 42% measured, over what period, and what was your specific contribution versus the rest of the team's?
Strong answerNames the baseline the 42% was measured against, the time window, and how it was tracked, then draws an honest line between his work and the team's. Comfortable saying "this part was mine, that part wasn't" instead of claiming the whole figure.
Red flagsCan't explain how the 42% was actually measured, or the baseline keeps moving. It's "we" for everything, and the number loses definition — or quietly shrinks — the moment you ask a second question.
Standout number — automation
4You automated the weekly client-reporting pipeline and saved "~300 analyst-hours a month." Walk me through exactly what you built, what it replaced, and how you arrived at 300 hours.
Strong answerWalks through the real mechanics — what tools, what manual process it replaced, who used the output — and shows the 300 hours has arithmetic behind it (analysts × hours × frequency), not a round number. It's clear he built or drove it, not just requested it.
Red flagsHand-waves the "how" and can't reconstruct where 300 came from — it's a tidy guess. Ownership gets fuzzy: it starts to sound like someone else built it and he's claiming the outcome.
Vague claim → concrete ownership
5You wrote "led cross-functional initiatives across product, engineering and data." Pick one. Tell me exactly what you owned, who you had to get aligned, and the moment it nearly fell apart.
Strong answerPicks one concrete initiative, names the actual people and teams he had to align, and describes a real point of tension and how he worked through it. Uses "I" for what he drove and "we" only where it's genuinely earned.
Red flagsStays at the altitude of the resume line — "drove alignment," "got everyone on the same page" — with no specific people or moment of friction. Real cross-functional work always has some; a story with none usually means he watched rather than led.
