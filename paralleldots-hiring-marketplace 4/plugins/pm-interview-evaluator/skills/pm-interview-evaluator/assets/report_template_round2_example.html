1 · Case study performance — ShelfWatch delivery recovery (ABC Corp)

Case study brief given to the candidate

An ongoing ShelfWatch multi-market rollout is 4 months late and 25% over budget from client scope creep; the sponsor is frustrated and evaluating another vendor. Deliverables: a data-driven “red” status narrative, a recovery plan, and a project-governance strategy.

Round 2 case study score
7.3 / 10 — straight average of the six case factors
One of the two inputs to the combined final score. Detailed performance against each metric below.

Performance against each metric

8
/ 10

Recovery plan quality

Built a re-baselined 6-week recovery plan that front-loaded quick wins (re-annotating the two worst-performing markets) before the harder store-coverage backfill, and identified the critical path correctly with a buffer tied to the client’s board review. The one gap: he didn’t quantify the cost of the recovery itself, so the budget-overrun conversation stayed partly open.

7
/ 10

Project governance

Proposed a sensible governance layer — a formal change-control log, a weekly steering call with the sponsor, and a RAID review cadence. Where it thinned out was decision rights: he didn’t specify who can approve scope changes or trigger an escalation, so the model would still lean on him personally. Solid structure, light on the authority detail.

8
/ 10

Data-driven status narrative

Framed the ‘red’ status with the actual burn-down and a scope-creep waterfall showing the 25% overage came from client-added SKUs, not delivery slippage — exactly the right move with a frustrated sponsor. Led with data and a path forward rather than excuses. Could have pre-empted the ‘why wasn’t this flagged sooner’ question, but the core narrative was strong.

7
/ 10

Difficult client conversations

In the role-play he opened the reset directly and calmly, acknowledged the delay without over-apologising, and re-anchored on shared goals — holding up well under the sponsor’s pressure. The soft spot: when the sponsor pushed to add more scope ‘to make up for it,’ he half-accepted rather than holding the line and trading it against the timeline.

6
/ 10

Team & cross-team management

Handled the mid-case key resignation reasonably — redistributed the critical annotation work and protected the timeline. But cross-team coordination stayed thin: he never described how he’d escalate when engineering pushes back on the recovery date, and there was no explicit plan to keep the wider delivery team motivated through a red project. The weakest area.

8
/ 10

Communication & presentation

The deck was well-sequenced — situation, root cause, recovery, governance — and he explained change-control and critical-path concepts in plain language a non-technical sponsor could follow. Confident, concise delivery; a genuinely client-ready presentation.

Why this recommendation

  • Combined score 7.3/10 → Hire band (7.0–7.9). Average of Round 1 (7.2) and Round 2 (7.3): (7.2 + 7.3) ÷ 2 = 7.25, rounded to 7.3. A solid hire, short of the 8.0 Strong-hire bar.
  • Strong where it counts. Recovery plan (8) and data-driven status narrative (8) were the core of the case, and the live client reset (7) closed exactly the client-handling gap flagged in Round 1.
  • Minor reservation 1 — cross-team escalation. Team & cross-team management was the lowest case factor (6): the key-resignation re-plan was fine, but the tech-team escalation path stayed thin. Worth an onboarding focus.
  • Minor reservation 2 — governance depth. Governance decision-rights (Round 2) and the untested cross-functional depth from Round 1 mean he should be paired with a strong delivery lead on his first multi-market account.

Interviewer Performance

4/ 5
Interviewer: Priya Nair · Round 2

Scored on how well the case study surfaced signal on the high-weight axes. The exercise put real pressure on client-handling and delivery recovery — the joint-highest dimensions — and the role-play forced a genuine hard conversation. Held back from a 5 only because cross-team escalation was never explicitly probed, leaving the lowest factor under-tested.

What went well

  • Targeted the Round 1 gap. The delivery-recovery case was the right pick — it directly tested the client-pressure axis that Round 1 left unproven.
  • Pushed the hard conversation live. The sponsor role-play didn’t let him retreat to process; it forced a real reset and exposed how he holds a line.
  • Insisted on data. Asking him to defend the ‘red’ status with numbers surfaced clean, non-defensive framing.

What was missed / to improve

  • Cross-team escalation went unprobed. The key-resignation scenario was raised but the tech-team escalation path wasn’t pressed, leaving the weakest facet (6) thinly evidenced.
    Could have asked: “Engineering says the recovery date is impossible — walk me through exactly how you escalate and to whom.”
    To improve: when a facet looks soft mid-exercise, drill it before moving on rather than at the end.
  • Governance stayed high-level. Decision rights were accepted without a concrete example of who signs off on scope changes. To improve: ask for one worked example of the governance model in action.
